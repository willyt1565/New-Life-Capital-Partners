If you served in the United States military, there is a real estate investing tool available to you that most financial advisors won't tell you about — because most financial advisors don't know about it themselves.

It's called the VA loan. You've probably heard of it. You may have even used one to buy your home. But there's a version of the VA loan strategy that can generate rental income, build equity, and kickstart a real estate portfolio — and it requires zero dollars down.

Let's break it down.

What Makes the VA Loan Different
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Before we get to strategy, here's why the VA loan is exceptional compared to conventional financing:

▶ BOLD → No down payment required.
Conventional investment property loans typically require 20–25% down. On a $200,000 property, that's $40,000–$50,000 out of pocket before you even begin. VA loans eliminate that barrier entirely for eligible veterans and service members.

▶ BOLD → No private mortgage insurance (PMI).
Conventional loans with less than 20% down charge PMI — an ongoing monthly fee that adds hundreds of dollars to your payment and generates zero equity. VA loans don't have it.

▶ BOLD → Competitive interest rates.
VA loans consistently offer some of the lowest mortgage rates on the market because they're backed by the federal government. Lower rates mean lower payments and more cash flow.

▶ BOLD → More flexible credit requirements.
VA loans are more forgiving on credit history than conventional loans, giving veterans with imperfect credit a legitimate path to homeownership and investing.

The trade-off: VA loans are intended for primary residences. You have to live in the property. But that's where the strategy gets interesting.

The Multi-Unit Strategy: Live in One, Rent the Rest
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Here's what most veterans don't know: VA loans can be used to purchase multi-unit properties — up to four units — as long as you live in one of them.

This is called house hacking, and it's one of the most powerful wealth-building strategies available to anyone, let alone veterans with zero-down financing.

Here's how it works:

You purchase a duplex, triplex, or four-unit property using your VA loan. You move into one unit. You rent out the remaining units. The rental income from your tenants covers part — or in many cases, all — of your mortgage payment.

▶ BOLD → A real example:
A veteran purchases a four-unit property for $280,000 using a VA loan. Monthly mortgage payment: approximately $1,650 (at a competitive VA rate). The three other units rent for $750 each, generating $2,250 in monthly rental income. After the mortgage, the veteran is cash-flow positive by $600/month — and living rent-free.

After a year (the typical minimum occupancy requirement), the veteran can move out, continue renting all four units, and repeat the process with another VA loan — or convert the equity into a new deal.

VA Loan Eligibility: Who Qualifies
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

To use a VA loan, you must meet service requirements. Generally, you qualify if:

[START BULLET LIST]
You served 90 consecutive days of active duty during wartime
You served 181 days of active duty during peacetime
You have more than 6 years of service in the National Guard or Reserves
You are the spouse of a service member who died in the line of duty or from a service-connected disability
[END BULLET LIST]

The best way to confirm eligibility is to request your Certificate of Eligibility (COE) through the VA or through a VA-approved lender. This takes minutes online.

The VA Funding Fee
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

One cost to be aware of: the VA charges a one-time funding fee, which is a percentage of the loan amount (typically 2.15% for first-time use with no down payment). This fee can be rolled into the loan — meaning you don't pay it upfront — but it does add to your overall loan balance.

Veterans with a service-connected disability rating of 10% or higher are exempt from the funding fee entirely. If that's you, this benefit becomes even more powerful.

How to Use Your VA Loan Benefit More Than Once
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Many veterans assume the VA loan is a one-time benefit. It isn't.

Your VA loan entitlement can be restored once you sell the property and pay off the loan, or once you've paid off a prior VA loan in full. Some veterans also have remaining entitlement after their first use, which allows them to carry two VA loans simultaneously in certain circumstances.

Working with a VA-experienced lender is essential here — the rules around entitlement and restoration are specific, and the right lender will walk you through your options clearly.

Beyond the VA Loan: Other Low-Down Options for First Responders
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Not every reader has VA eligibility — and not every deal fits the owner-occupied requirement. Here are two additional programs worth knowing:

▶ BOLD → FHA Loans (3.5% down):
Available to anyone who qualifies. FHA loans allow multi-unit purchases (up to 4 units) with owner occupancy, similar to the VA strategy. The down payment is 3.5% with a credit score of 580 or higher. FHA does require PMI, but at 3.5% down, the entry barrier is still dramatically lower than conventional investment financing.

▶ BOLD → Good Neighbor Next Door Program:
This HUD program offers a 50% discount on listed homes in designated revitalization areas for law enforcement officers, firefighters, EMTs, and teachers. The catch: you must live in the property for 36 months. But a 50% discount on acquisition cost is an extraordinary head start on equity.

The Practical Steps to Get Started

The Bigger Picture
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The VA loan is one of the most generous benefits the United States government offers veterans — and it's consistently underutilized. Many veterans use it once for a primary home and never revisit what else it can do.

The multi-unit strategy turns a housing benefit into a wealth-building engine. You live for free (or near-free). You build equity through tenant payments. You generate cash flow. And you gain experience as a landlord that positions you for your next acquisition.

This is how a real estate portfolio starts — not with a windfall or a lucky break, but with a strategic use of the resources already available to you.

You earned this benefit. Use it.
