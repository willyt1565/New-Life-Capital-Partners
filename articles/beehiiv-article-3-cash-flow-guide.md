Real estate investing has a reputation for being complicated. Walk into any conversation about it and you'll hear terms like cap rate, NOI, cash-on-cash return, gross rent multiplier, and debt service coverage ratio — and if nobody explains them clearly, most people tune out.

That's a mistake. Because these numbers aren't complicated. They're just math — the kind of clear, objective analysis you already use on the job every day.

This issue breaks down the three most important metrics you need to evaluate any rental property. By the end, you'll be able to look at a deal and know within minutes whether it's worth pursuing further.

The Foundation: What Cash Flow Actually Is
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Cash flow is the money left over each month after you've paid every expense associated with owning the property.

It sounds simple because it is:

▶ BOLD → Cash Flow = Total Rental Income − Total Expenses

If you collect $1,800/month in rent and your total monthly expenses are $1,400, your cash flow is $400/month — or $4,800/year.

Positive cash flow means the property is paying you. Negative cash flow means you're paying the property. The goal, especially starting out, is positive.

Here's what goes into "total expenses":

[START BULLET LIST]
Mortgage payment (principal + interest)
Property taxes
Insurance
Property management fees (if applicable)
Vacancy allowance (budget 5–10% of rent for months the property sits empty)
Maintenance and repairs (budget 5–10% of rent)
Capital expenditure reserves (budget for big-ticket replacements: roof, HVAC, water heater)
[END BULLET LIST]

Most beginner investors underestimate expenses. Run conservative numbers. If the deal still works on conservative projections, it works.

Metric #1: Net Operating Income (NOI)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Net Operating Income is your annual income from the property before accounting for your mortgage payment.

▶ BOLD → NOI = Annual Gross Rent − Operating Expenses (excluding mortgage)

Why exclude the mortgage? Because NOI measures the property's performance independent of how you financed it. Two investors can own identical properties — one paid cash, one took a loan — and both should arrive at the same NOI. The financing is a separate variable.

▶ BOLD → Example:

[START BULLET LIST]
Annual gross rent: $21,600 ($1,800/month)
Operating expenses (taxes, insurance, management, maintenance, vacancy): $7,200/year
NOI = $21,600 − $7,200 = $14,400/year
[END BULLET LIST]

NOI is the starting point for most other calculations. Get comfortable with it.

Metric #2: Cap Rate (Capitalization Rate)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The cap rate tells you the rate of return a property generates if you paid for it entirely in cash — no mortgage, no financing.

▶ BOLD → Cap Rate = NOI ÷ Property Value × 100

Using our example:

[START BULLET LIST]
NOI: $14,400
Property value: $180,000
Cap Rate = $14,400 ÷ $180,000 × 100 = 8%
[END BULLET LIST]

An 8% cap rate means the property generates 8 cents in annual income for every dollar of value.

▶ BOLD → What's a good cap rate? It depends on the market:

[START BULLET LIST]
In high-cost cities like New York or San Francisco, cap rates of 3–5% are common — prices are high relative to rents
In mid-sized and growing markets, 6–9% cap rates are typical and desirable
In rural or smaller markets, cap rates of 10% or higher may be achievable — but a higher cap rate can also signal higher risk
[END BULLET LIST]

Cap rate is most useful for comparing properties in the same market. A 7% cap rate in Baton Rouge tells you more when you compare it to other Baton Rouge properties than when you compare it to a property in Dallas.

One important note: cap rate doesn't account for your mortgage. It tells you about the property's income potential — not your personal return on investment with financing. For that, you need the next metric.

Metric #3: Cash-on-Cash Return (CoC)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Cash-on-cash return measures the actual return you're getting on the money you put into the deal — your down payment, closing costs, and any initial repairs.

▶ BOLD → Cash-on-Cash Return = Annual Cash Flow ÷ Total Cash Invested × 100

▶ BOLD → Example:

[START BULLET LIST]
Purchase price: $180,000
Down payment (20%): $36,000
Closing costs + initial repairs: $6,000
Total cash invested: $42,000
[END BULLET LIST]

With a mortgage in place, let's say monthly cash flow (after all expenses including the mortgage) is $350:

[START BULLET LIST]
Annual cash flow: $4,200
CoC Return = $4,200 ÷ $42,000 × 100 = 10%
[END BULLET LIST]

A 10% cash-on-cash return means you're earning 10 cents annually for every dollar you invested. Compare that to a savings account at 4–5%, or the stock market's historical average of 7–10% — and real estate begins to look very competitive, especially with the added benefits of equity buildup and appreciation on top of that return.

▶ BOLD → What's a good cash-on-cash return? Most experienced investors target 8–12%+. Below 6% may not justify the time and risk. Above 15% in a stable market is exceptional and worth investigating closely (high returns can also mean hidden problems).

Putting It Together: A Quick Deal Analysis
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Here's how these three numbers work together on a real property evaluation.

▶ BOLD → Property: 3-bedroom single-family home
▶ BOLD → Purchase price: $155,000
▶ BOLD → Expected monthly rent: $1,400

▶ BOLD → Step 1 — Estimate operating expenses:

[START BULLET LIST]
Property taxes: $150/month
Insurance: $80/month
Property management (10%): $140/month
Vacancy allowance (8%): $112/month
Maintenance + CapEx reserves (10%): $140/month
Total monthly expenses (ex-mortgage): $622
Annual operating expenses: $7,464
[END BULLET LIST]

▶ BOLD → Step 2 — Calculate NOI:

[START BULLET LIST]
Annual gross rent: $16,800
Annual operating expenses: $7,464
NOI = $9,336
[END BULLET LIST]

▶ BOLD → Step 3 — Calculate Cap Rate:

[START BULLET LIST]
Cap Rate = $9,336 ÷ $155,000 × 100 = 6.02%
[END BULLET LIST]

▶ BOLD → Step 4 — Calculate Cash-on-Cash Return:

[START BULLET LIST]
Down payment (20%): $31,000
Closing costs: $4,000
Total cash invested: $35,000
Monthly mortgage payment (30-yr, ~7% rate): ~$826
Monthly cash flow: $1,400 − $622 − $826 = −$48/month
[END BULLET LIST]

This property at these numbers is slightly cash-flow negative — you'd be paying $48/month out of pocket. Is that a dealbreaker? Not necessarily. If the market appreciates and rent increases, that turns positive quickly. But as a starting investor, you'd want to negotiate the price down, find a higher-rent property, or put more down to reduce the mortgage payment.

This is how you evaluate a deal in 20 minutes — not by feeling, by math.

The One-Percent Rule: A Quick Gut Check
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Before running full numbers, experienced investors use the 1% Rule as a quick filter:

▶ BOLD → Monthly rent should be at least 1% of the purchase price.

[START BULLET LIST]
$155,000 property → needs $1,550/month rent to pass the 1% rule
$155,000 property renting at $1,400 → fails the 1% rule (at 0.9%)
[END BULLET LIST]

The 1% rule is not a guarantee of cash flow — it's a filter to quickly eliminate properties not worth analyzing further. In high-cost markets, finding 1% deals is difficult. In mid-size markets like many areas of the South, it's achievable.

Use it to narrow the field, then run the full numbers on the survivors.

The Bottom Line
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

You don't need an MBA to evaluate a rental property. You need three numbers — NOI, cap rate, and cash-on-cash return — and the discipline to calculate them honestly without letting emotion skew the math.

That discipline is something first responders have in abundance. The difference between a good deal and a bad deal isn't usually luck — it's whether someone did the math before they signed.
